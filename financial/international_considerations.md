# International Considerations

**Purpose:** If you're applying from outside the US and considering moving for a PhD, this covers the practical visa, banking, healthcare, and cultural-transition logistics. Even if you're already in the US, many of these apply if you move to a Europe program.

**Audience assumption:** the applicant (John Paul L. Gabule) may be a non-US national applying primarily to US PhD programs. Adapt sections based on actual citizenship.

---

## US visa pathway (most common: F-1)

**F-1 student visa** is the standard for international PhD students in the US.

### Timeline (typical, from acceptance to arrival):

| Step | Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Accept admission offer | by Apr 15, 2028 | US PhD common-reply date |
| Receive I-20 from program | 2-6 weeks after acceptance | Program's international office handles |
| Pay SEVIS fee | After receiving I-20 | $350 (2026 rate; verify) |
| Schedule visa interview | 2-12 weeks wait | Varies by country; some countries have multi-month waits |
| Visa interview | 30-90 days before move | At US consulate in your country |
| Visa issued | 1-7 days after interview | Stamp in passport |
| Travel to US | Up to 30 days before program start | F-1 entry window |

**Plan for delays.** US consulate wait times in 2026 are 2-8 weeks in many countries. Schedule the interview the day you receive your I-20.

### Documents needed:
- Form DS-160 (online application)
- Form I-20 (from program)
- SEVIS fee receipt
- Passport (valid 6+ months past program start)
- Financial documentation (proof you can fund yourself OR full funding letter from program)
- Academic transcripts
- Visa interview appointment confirmation

### Cost (approximate, 2026):
- DS-160 fee: $185
- SEVIS fee: $350
- Visa appointment fee: included in DS-160 most consulates
- Optional: legal consultation $300-1,000

### Common reasons F-1 visas get denied:
- "Failure to demonstrate ties to home country" (most common; you need to show you intend to return after PhD even if you might stay)
- Inconsistencies between DS-160 and supporting documents
- Insufficient financial documentation
- Past visa violations

### If denied:
- You can re-apply, but each re-application is a new fee
- Don't lie or change your story
- Consider a US immigration lawyer ($500-2,000) for the re-application

## Europe PhD as an international applicant

European PhDs are typically structured as employment contracts (you're a "research employee," not a "student" in the US sense). This changes the visa pathway substantially.

### Typical structure by country:

**Switzerland (ETH, EPFL):**
- B-permit for non-EU/EFTA nationals
- Permit follows the employment contract from the university
- Higher stipend (CHF 80k-95k) but high cost of living (Zürich is one of the most expensive cities in the world)
- ~5-week processing once contract is signed

**UK (Cambridge, Oxford, Edinburgh, UCL):**
- Student visa (Tier 4 / Student Route) for stipend-based PhDs
- Skilled Worker visa for employment-based research positions
- Healthcare: NHS access included
- Cost: visa fee ~£490 + Immigration Health Surcharge ~£1,035/year

**Germany (TUM, Heidelberg):**
- Research Visa or Job Seeker Visa initially
- Generally easier for STEM PhDs
- Free or very low tuition; modest stipend (€18k-32k)
- 18-month post-PhD job-search visa

**Netherlands (UvA):**
- Highly skilled migrant visa via the university sponsor
- Modest stipend (€28k-36k)
- 30% ruling tax break for international hires (significant)

### Process:
- University handles most of the paperwork once contract is signed
- Allow 6-12 weeks for visa processing
- Some Schengen countries (Germany, Netherlands) allow 90-day visa-free entry to attend interviews

## Banking, taxes, and money management

### US-specific:
- Open a US bank account within 30 days of arrival (Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, or a credit union near the campus)
- ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) within first 90 days if not eligible for SSN
- Apply for SSN if F-1 with on-campus employment (most PhDs qualify via TA / RA)
- Taxes: F-1 students filed as non-resident aliens for 5 years; use Sprintax or similar
- Most international students have tax treaties with US (China, India, UK, Korea, etc.) — saves real money

### EU-specific:
- Open a local bank account within first month
- Tax registration with the relevant authority
- Many programs have "welcome week" support for paperwork

### Currency exchange:
- Don't transfer large sums at the airport (bad rates)
- Use Wise (formerly TransferWise) or Revolut for international transfers — much better rates than banks
- Set up automatic small transfers monthly rather than infrequent large transfers

## Healthcare

### US:
- F-1 students must have health insurance; most programs require their own plan (~$2,000-3,500/year deducted from stipend)
- Mental health access: most universities have free counseling for currently-enrolled students; supplement with insurance-covered therapy if needed
- Dental and vision usually NOT included in basic plan; budget $50-100/month for separate plans

### UK:
- NHS access included after paying the Immigration Health Surcharge
- GP registration is your first step on arrival

### Switzerland:
- Mandatory private insurance (KVG); typically CHF 300-500/month
- Higher quality than US care, lower out-of-pocket

### General:
- Get a comprehensive checkup in your home country before departure
- Bring 3-6 month supply of any prescription medications (verify legality of importing)
- Arrange dental work in home country if cost-conscious

## Cultural and practical transition

### Things people don't tell you:

**Loneliness in year 1.** Almost everyone experiences this regardless of program. Buffer: lean into the cohort (your fellow first-year PhDs); attend social events for the first 6 months even when you don't feel like it.

**Imposter syndrome.** Particularly common for international students. You were admitted because the committee saw evidence you can do the work. The first year is for catching up on context, not for proving you belong.

**Language fatigue (if English is not native):** even fluent English speakers report cognitive fatigue from running an entire research life in a second language. Take rest days seriously.

**Food and groceries:** budget 1-2 weeks for adjustment. Most universities have international student associations that help newcomers find specialty grocery stores.

**Time zone disorientation:** ~2 weeks if moving across multiple time zones. Don't make big decisions in week 1.

### Cultural communication norms (US-specific, may apply to UK):

- **Directness in feedback.** US academic culture is generally more direct than e.g., East Asian or Northern European academic culture. Feedback that seems harsh may be normal.
- **Self-promotion.** Many international students underweight US norms around self-promotion. You'll need to advocate for your work in seminars, in meetings, and in writing.
- **Networking.** Less hierarchical than some cultures. You can introduce yourself to senior researchers; they expect it.
- **Casual interactions.** Lunch with advisor / lab is common; it's working time, not socializing.

## Bringing a partner / family

If you have a partner or dependents:

### F-2 visa (spouse / dependent children of F-1):
- F-2 spouse cannot work in the US
- F-2 children under 21 can attend K-12 school
- Often a financial strain if the spouse was previously employed

### F-1 OPT and the partner path:
- After completing the PhD, F-1 can stay on OPT (Optional Practical Training) for 1-3 years (CS = STEM extension)
- During OPT, options to transition to H-1B / O-1 / EB visas
- The spouse on F-2 has limited options until you transition

### Long-distance reality:
- Many international PhD students have partners who stay in home country, especially for the first 1-2 years
- Plan for at least 3-4 visits per year if doing long-distance
- The 18-month plan + 5-year PhD = 6.5 years of potential separation; have this conversation explicitly before applying

### Europe spouse policies:
- Generally more spouse-friendly than US
- Switzerland: B-permit dependents can work; ETH offers childcare support
- UK: spouse on Student Dependent visa can usually work
- Netherlands: highly-skilled migrant program covers dependents

## OPT / post-PhD pathways (US)

If admitted to a US PhD, plan for post-PhD options early.

### OPT (Optional Practical Training):
- 12 months standard
- 24-month STEM extension for qualifying CS PhDs (extends total to 36 months)
- Begin planning in year 4 of the PhD; OPT application before graduation is common

### H-1B:
- Random lottery; outcomes uncertain
- Most CS PhDs win lottery within 1-2 cycles given specialty preference
- Some employers cover legal fees and time

### O-1 (extraordinary ability):
- Higher bar but no lottery
- Reasonable for PhDs with publications + recognized contributions
- Many top labs / academic positions sponsor O-1

### EB-1A (extraordinary ability green card):
- The most prestigious path
- Realistic for top PhD graduates with strong publication record + citations
- Plan early (year 3-4); the application takes 6-18 months

## Country-of-origin specific resources

- **India / China / Korea:** large existing PhD student communities at all US top programs; use the alumni networks
- **EU / UK:** generally easier US visa path due to lower fraud-flag rate; bilateral tax treaties are favorable
- **Philippines / SE Asia:** smaller cohorts at top US programs; lean into the larger US graduate alumni associations
- **Latin America:** OAS scholarships sometimes available; check before applying

## Closing checklist for an international applicant

Before applying:
- [ ] Verify your passport is valid through Aug 2030 (extends past program start + buffer)
- [ ] Research visa pathway for top 3 target countries
- [ ] Identify any country-specific tax treaty benefits
- [ ] Talk to current PhD students from your country at your target programs (most are happy to share)
- [ ] Confirm with family / partner about long-term plan

After acceptance (April 2028):
- [ ] Begin visa application within 1 week of accepting
- [ ] Open communication channel with program's international office
- [ ] Arrange housing in advance (most universities have international student housing; book early)
- [ ] Set up banking, healthcare, phone plan logistics for arrival
- [ ] Reserve buffer time (1-2 weeks) between arrival and program start

After arrival (August/September 2028):
- [ ] Register with international office on day 1
- [ ] Open bank account in week 1
- [ ] Register for healthcare in week 1
- [ ] Get local phone number in week 1
- [ ] Attend orientation events (most are required anyway)

## A note for non-international applicants

If you're a US citizen / permanent resident applying to US programs: skip most of this file. Most of the visa, healthcare, and cultural-transition burden does not apply. Focus on the financial-planning side (`financial/stipend_planning.md`) and the offer-evaluation side (`decisions/offer_evaluation.md`).

If applying to Europe programs, much of this file applies in reverse: you'll go through the equivalent processes in the destination country. The general principles (early planning, visa is the constraint, build buffer time) all transfer.
