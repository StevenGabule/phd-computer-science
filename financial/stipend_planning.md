# PhD Financial Planning

**Purpose:** Realistic financial picture of PhD stipends + cost of living across your target programs, so you can compare offers in April 2028 with informed eyes.

**Caveat:** all numbers below are 2026 baselines; verify against actual 2028 offer letters. Stipends rise ~3-5% per year at most institutions; cost of living rises faster in some areas (Bay Area, NYC).

---

## What a PhD CS stipend looks like (US, 2026 baselines)

| Tier | Annual stipend | Typical hours expected | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top US (Stanford, MIT, CMU, Berkeley, UW) | $45,000-55,000 | 20 hr/week TA OR full RA | Often 12-month, sometimes 9-month |
| Mid US (Princeton, Cornell, UMich, UMD) | $35,000-45,000 | 20 hr/week TA OR full RA | Often 12-month |
| Lower-tier R1 US | $28,000-38,000 | TA-heavy | Sometimes 9-month only |
| Europe (general) | €18,000-32,000 (net) | Full-time research position | Employee status with benefits |
| ETH / EPFL | CHF 80,000-95,000 (gross) | Full-time research position | Highest paid PhD in Europe |
| UK (general) | £19,000-23,000 (net of taxes) | Full-time research student | UKRI minimum standard |

**Tax treatment varies:**
- US: federal income tax applies; some states no income tax (TX, FL, WA); some high (CA, NY)
- Europe: salary-based PhDs are taxed like employees; stipend-based are sometimes tax-free
- UK: stipends are tax-free

## Comparing offers fairly

When offers come in (April 2028), build a per-program comparison spreadsheet:

| Column | What |
|---|---|
| Program | name |
| Annual stipend | gross |
| Stipend duration | 9-month or 12-month |
| Summer funding | guaranteed / available / not provided |
| Multi-year guarantee | first year only / multi-year / unconditional |
| Tuition | covered? (almost always yes for US PhDs; verify Europe) |
| Health insurance | covered? |
| Conference travel budget | typical $/year |
| Computing resources | shared cluster / personal allocation / Colab-only |
| Estimated COL (rent) | per city |
| Net discretionary | stipend - rent - basic expenses |
| Tax treatment | rough net income |

The "best offer" is rarely the highest stipend. Stipend × COL × duration matters more.

## Hidden costs of a PhD

Things not in the stipend but you'll pay for:

| Cost | Annual | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Conference travel beyond covered amount | $0-3,000 | Often >2 conferences/year if active |
| Visa fees + lawyer (international students) | $500-3,000 first year, $200-500/year after | Varies dramatically by country |
| Moving costs (initial relocation) | $1,500-5,000 | Sometimes partially reimbursed |
| Software / tools not covered by institution | $0-500 | Most universities provide site licenses |
| Books and reference materials | $200-500 | Most journals via institutional access |
| Health expenses beyond insurance | $500-2,000 | Co-pays, dental, vision often partial |
| Professional clothing for conferences | $200-500 | One-time then maintenance |
| Personal computer if institution doesn't provide | $1,500-3,000 | Most don't; you'll want your own |

**Reserve fund recommendation:** start the PhD with ~$5,000-8,000 in savings as a buffer. If you can't, take out a small federal loan for year 1 if you're eligible.

## Loans during a PhD

**General principle:** funded PhDs don't require loans. If a program asks you to take loans to cover basic costs, that's a red flag — the program isn't actually funding you adequately.

**Exception:** federal subsidized loans for international students from countries with restricted access to home-country loans, or for one-time relocation costs. Don't fund recurring expenses with loans.

**Anti-pattern:** taking on substantial loan debt for a PhD. The salary trajectory after a CS PhD is good but not immediate — you'll be 30-32 graduating with the PhD; you don't want to also be repaying loans.

## Side income during the PhD

Most programs allow some external income; some restrict.

**Common patterns:**
- **Summer internships at industry research labs.** $25k-50k for 12 weeks at Google / Meta / Microsoft / Anthropic. Most PhD students do 1-2 of these.
- **Consulting (limited):** some programs allow up to 10-20% time on external consulting; verify rules at your specific program.
- **Open-source contracting:** rare but exists; usually tied to a specific project.
- **Teaching extra courses:** some programs let you TA a second course for additional pay.

**What to avoid:**
- Full-time work alongside PhD (your research will suffer)
- Side projects that compete with your PhD topic (IP issues + reputational issues)
- Anything that violates the advisor's understanding of how you'll spend your time

## The "I might not get in" financial scenario

If all programs reject (low probability but real):
- The application costs (~$2,500-3,500) are sunk
- The 18-month work is not sunk — the artifacts (paper, benchmark, code) persist
- Next cycle: re-apply to a refined program list; many applicants get in on the second cycle with stronger materials
- Interim: most CiteCheck-relevant industry roles (research engineer at AI labs, legal-tech startups) are reasonably accessible with the artifacts you'll have

The financial downside is bounded. Don't let financial anxiety distort the program-selection decision in April 2028.

## Stipend-negotiation reality check

US PhD stipends are mostly NOT negotiable. The standard offer is what the program offers. Exceptions:
- Top-tier candidates (NSF GRFP awardees) sometimes get a stipend top-up
- Fellowship awards (Hertz, OpenAI, Anthropic) come with substantial stipends; programs often allow you to take both
- Some programs have a "named fellowship" boost for top candidates

What you CAN often negotiate:
- Start date flexibility
- Summer funding for year 1
- Relocation reimbursement
- Computing allocation
- Conference travel allowance

If you have a competing offer, you can mention it diplomatically — but don't pretend to have offers you don't have.

---

## Closing

PhD financial planning is mostly about: choosing a program where the stipend × COL × duration leaves you a sustainable life. The differences between programs are real but usually not the deciding factor; advisor fit and research direction matter more (see `decisions/offer_evaluation.md`).

Plan to be financially constrained for 4-6 years. The post-PhD payoff is real (CS PhDs typically earn $200k+ in industry research roles or are well-positioned for faculty), but the during-PhD years require discipline.

Don't take on debt. Don't take an offer where you can't make rent + groceries + occasional travel work. Don't compare yourself to peers earning $200k+ at FAANG — you chose a different path with different rewards.
