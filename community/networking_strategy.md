# Networking Strategy

**Purpose:** Build the research-community presence that turns a solo PhD-prep cycle into a connected one. Doesn't require extroversion; does require intentionality.

**Why this matters:** ~30-40% of PhD admits to top programs cite "knew the lab from previous interaction" as a contributing factor. The interactions don't have to be deep; they have to exist.

---

## The three rings

Networking happens at three concentric scales. Develop all three:

| Ring | What | Where | Time investment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inner | 1-2 people you exchange paper drafts with | Reading group, paper-swap | 2-3 hrs/week |
| Middle | 5-10 people who recognize you in their field | Twitter/Bluesky, conferences | 30-60 min/week |
| Outer | 50-200 people who follow your work | Public artifacts, blog | passive after setup |

You don't need a "personal brand." You need to be findable, with substance.

---

## Inner ring — paper-swap partners

**Goal:** find 1-2 people who'll read your drafts and let you read theirs.

**Where to find them:**
- Reading group (the easiest path; just ask after a meeting)
- A peer in a different program working on adjacent problems (ACL Slack, ML Collective Discord)
- A PhD student you connected with at a workshop (even online workshops work)
- Twitter / Bluesky: respond thoughtfully to 5 people's threads, then DM one with "I'm working on adjacent stuff; would you be open to a draft swap?"

**The mechanics:**
- One sends a draft (paper section, SOP, problem statement); the other responds within 1 week with feedback
- Feedback format: 2-3 things that work, 2-3 things that don't, 1 question that would push the thinking
- Reciprocity matters; if you receive feedback, give it back within 2 weeks

**Anti-pattern:** asking for feedback from people who don't need yours. The relationship dies. Mutual benefit only.

---

## Middle ring — academic Twitter / Bluesky

**Goal:** 5-10 senior researchers in your area know your handle.

**Setup (one-time, 1 hour):**
- Create a Twitter/X and Bluesky account with your real name
- Bio: "PhD applicant — [research area]. [Project URL]"
- Avatar: a clear photo of your face (not a logo, cartoon, or anime character — this matters)
- Pinned post: 1-paragraph description of your current research project (CiteCheck)
- Follow: 100-200 researchers in your area, plus 50 broader-CS / ML accounts

**Cadence (30 minutes/week max):**
- Post 1-2 things per week:
  - A paper you're reading + a thoughtful takeaway (NOT just "interesting paper")
  - A small artifact you shipped (eyecite PR merged, a blog post, a notebook)
  - A question that doesn't have an obvious answer (often gets more responses than statements)
- Respond to 3-5 senior researchers' posts per week, in a substantive way (not just "👍" or "great paper")
- Don't post hot takes about hot topics. Don't post about politics. Don't post anything you wouldn't put on a CV.

**What works:**
- Specificity > generality ("Reuter 2025's chunking strategy for legal corpora" > "RAG is cool")
- Questions > assertions ("Has anyone tried X with Y?" > "X with Y is the obvious move")
- Following up on conversations ("@user mentioned [thing] last week; I tried it and..." builds memory)

**What doesn't work:**
- Self-promotion without substance
- Calling people out for bad work
- Long threads on every topic ("expert generalist" is a bad look)
- Replying to threads with "this!" or "+1"

**Anti-pattern:** mistaking Twitter activity for actual progress. 30 min/week is the cap. If you find yourself scrolling for hours, log out for a week.

## Outer ring — public artifacts

**Goal:** when someone Googles your name, they find substantive things.

**Setup (~10 hours total across Phase 1):**

1. **Personal website (1-2 hours):** A simple page with name, bio paragraph, research direction, links to artifacts. Use GitHub Pages with Jekyll or Hugo. Don't optimize design — substance over polish.

2. **GitHub profile (30 min):** Pin your top 3 repos. Write a paragraph in your profile README about what you work on. Make sure your real name appears.

3. **Google Scholar profile (15 min):** Create one even if you don't have publications yet. Add any workshop papers, preprints, or other listings.

4. **ORCID (10 min):** Free, takes 5 minutes. Linkable from Google Scholar and arXiv.

5. **Research blog (ongoing):** Use Hashnode, Medium, GitHub Pages, or your personal website. Post 1 substantive piece per month. Topics:
   - Lessons from deep-reading a paper
   - A small experiment you ran
   - A surprising thing you learned about your subfield
   - A response to or extension of a current paper

**What "substantive" means for a blog:**
- 1,000-2,500 words
- One specific claim, supported with evidence
- Code, figures, or both
- Honest about what you don't know

**Frequency:** 1 post per month is plenty. More frequent without substance is worse than less frequent with substance.

## Conferences

Even attending online conferences is high-leverage. Aim for 2-3 in Phase 1-3.

**Recommended for your area (CiteCheck / legal NLP / agentic RAG):**
- **NLLP @ EMNLP** — your target venue; attend before you submit
- **TrustNLP @ NAACL or EMNLP** — adjacent community
- **SIGIR** — retrieval, RAG
- **ACL or EMNLP main** — the broader NLP community

**Attending pattern:**
- Virtual registration is often free or low-cost ($50-200)
- Watch keynotes + 5-10 paper presentations in your area
- Use the conference's online platform to message authors of papers you found interesting
- Take notes in your `journal/conferences/` directory (create as needed)

**Attending in person (if budget allows):**
- 1 in-person conference in Phase 3 (post-paper-submission) is the right window
- Goals: meet 3-5 specific people, attend 3-5 talks deeply, present a poster if you have a workshop paper
- Stay for the social events; the hallway track is where networks form

**Anti-pattern:** trying to "network" with senior researchers at conferences without having something specific to say. Read 1-2 of their papers; have a specific question; introduce yourself with that question.

## Email outreach (already covered)

See `outreach/email_template.md` and `outreach/email_variants/`. Key reminders:
- Personalize per advisor (cite a specific paper, comment on a specific contribution)
- 200-300 words max
- Explicit out ("if you can't respond, no worries")
- Send Tuesday or Wednesday morning recipient-time

Cold-email response rate is 10-20%. Don't take silence personally. Send 30+ to get 3-5 responses; that's the typical ratio.

## Reading groups

Reading groups are the single highest-leverage networking environment for a PhD-prep applicant.

**Why:**
- You meet 10-20 researchers per cycle
- Repeated exposure (weekly meetings) builds memory and rapport
- You get to see how senior researchers actually think
- You can volunteer to present (forcing function for deep reading)

**Where to find them:**
- **ML Collective** (mlcollective.org) — free, open, weekly
- **Cohere For AI** (cohere.com/research/cohere-for-ai) — free, focused
- **EleutherAI Discord** — multiple subgroup reading-discussion channels
- **University-affiliated groups** — many publish their meeting times publicly
- **ACL Special Interest Groups** (SIGNLL, SIGIR, etc.) — some have reading groups

**How to participate well:**

Weeks 1-3: listen. Don't speak unless asked. Watch how experienced researchers discuss papers.

Weeks 4-12: ask questions, don't make statements. "Did the authors discuss X?" is better than "I think they should have done Y."

After 3 months: volunteer to present a paper. The preparation forces deep reading; the presentation builds credibility.

After 6 months: propose a paper for the next meeting. Now you're shaping the group's direction.

## Discord / Slack communities

Specific communities worth joining:

- **ACL Slack** (large, somewhat noisy; good for finding paper-swap partners)
- **EleutherAI Discord** (active, technical, good for ML engineering questions)
- **Hugging Face Discord** (good for model-loading and benchmark questions)
- **Cohere For AI Discord** (active research community, smaller and friendlier)
- **CS @ Stanford Discord, MIT CSAIL Discord** etc. (some labs have public Discords; great for "would I fit here?" inference)

**Time investment:** 30 minutes/week, max. Browse channels, respond when you have something specific to add. Don't lurk in 20 servers.

## The "I'm not extroverted" objection

Most successful researchers are not extroverts. Networking is not "be charismatic"; it's "be findable and substantive."

The introvert-friendly networking stack:
- **Async over sync:** Twitter / Bluesky / Discord over conference small-talk
- **Specific over general:** "I read your paper on X" over "I'd love to hear about your work"
- **Written over spoken:** blog posts over hallway-track encounters
- **Small over large:** paper-swap (1-on-1) over large reading groups

You don't need to enjoy networking. You need to do enough of it to be findable.

---

## What NOT to do

- Email senior researchers asking to be "mentored"
- DM 50 people the same generic "I'm interested in your work" message
- Comment on every Twitter post to be visible (it's transparent)
- Pretend to have read papers you haven't
- Make grand statements you can't back up
- Engage in academic Twitter drama
- Use Twitter for political content during application cycle

## Cohort-building (peer networking)

The other PhD applicants in your cycle are not competition; they're your future cohort. Build relationships with them.

**Where to find them:**
- Reddit r/GradSchool, r/PhD
- The GradCafe forums (less active recently but still exists)
- Twitter (search for "applying to PhD CS Fall 2028")
- Reading groups (some members are also applicants)

**What to do with them:**
- Share program info you've gathered
- Compare notes on application strategies
- Read each other's SOPs (with permission)
- Provide moral support during the application push

**Anti-pattern:** competing on application count or perceived ranking. Not zero-sum; most years multiple cohort members get into the same program.

---

## A note on social media risk

Anything you post publicly is part of your application. Admissions committees Google candidates. What they find shapes their impression.

**Audit your existing presence:**
- Search your own name on Google, DuckDuckGo, Bing
- Check Twitter / X archive for the past 2 years
- Check old Reddit accounts (especially if you've posted controversial takes anywhere)

**Remove or privatize:**
- Anything political that could be polarizing
- Anything that suggests you don't take research seriously
- Anything that contradicts the picture you paint in your SOP

You don't have to be PR-perfect. You have to not have obvious red flags.

---

## A closing principle

Networking is not the goal; substance is. You network so that when you produce substance, someone notices. If you network without substance, the relationships fade. If you produce substance without networking, the work goes unnoticed.

The 18-month plan is 80% substance, 20% networking. Get the ratio right.
