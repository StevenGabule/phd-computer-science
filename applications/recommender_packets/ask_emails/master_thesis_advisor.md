# Ask Email — Master's Thesis Advisor

**Use case:** The recommender most likely to write the strongest letter — your master's thesis supervisor or equivalent. They have seen sustained work and can speak with detail to your research process, not just outcomes.

**Send timing:** May–July 2027 (6 months before earliest deadline of Dec 1, 2027). Earlier is better.

---

**To:** [Recommender's institutional email]
**Subject:** PhD letter of recommendation — Fall 2028 cycle — John Paul Gabule

---

Dear Professor [Last Name],

I hope this finds you well. I am writing to ask whether you would be willing to write a letter of recommendation for my PhD applications in the Fall 2028 cycle. The earliest deadlines are Dec 1, 2027, and I am preparing for about twenty programs across US and European universities.

The work I am applying to do builds directly on what we did together on my master's thesis on [SPECIFIC TOPIC]. My PhD research focus is **agentic retrieval-augmented generation for verifiable US case-law citations** — addressing the *Mata v. Avianca* problem where LLMs fabricate plausible-looking but non-existent legal citations. The system, called CiteCheck, combines a Bluebook-structure-aware reranker with a live-registry verification loop against the CourtListener API. I have spent 2026-2027 building the codebase, an open benchmark, and (target Sep 2026 arXiv) a citation-existence verifier mini-paper as a preliminary artifact. The full design is at https://github.com/StevenGabule/phd-computer-science.

I am asking you specifically because the [PERSONALIZE: e.g., "way you pushed me on the methodology of my thesis" / "feedback you gave on my [specific deliverable]"] is the single experience that shaped how I think about research, and committees will want to hear that perspective from someone who saw it firsthand. Of all my potential recommenders you have the deepest view of my sustained research capability.

If you can write a strong letter, I would be very grateful. **If for any reason you cannot — too little context, scheduling, or you would not be able to write strongly — please tell me.** I would rather know now and adjust my list than send a weaker letter into top-tier admissions committees. Either answer is genuinely fine.

I have a recommender packet ready (CV, draft SOP, accomplishments list, full program list with deadlines, Interfolio link, suggested talking points) that I will send as soon as you confirm. The packet is designed to make writing the letter as efficient as possible.

Even a one-line response in the next two weeks would be helpful so I can finalize my plans.

Thank you for considering this.

With gratitude,
John Paul L. Gabule
johnpaullimgabule@gmail.com

## Customization checklist before sending

- [ ] Replace `[Last Name]` with actual surname
- [ ] Replace `[SPECIFIC TOPIC]` with the actual master's thesis topic
- [ ] Replace `[PERSONALIZE: ...]` with a specific genuine memory of working together
- [ ] Verify the GitHub link is live (or omit until it is)
- [ ] Send Tuesday or Wednesday morning recipient-time (avoid Mondays / Fridays)
- [ ] Add a calendar reminder for 2-week follow-up if no response

## Notes

- This is the highest-stakes ask on the list. The master's advisor is also usually the strongest letter, so the cost of a weak version is high. Make the email as personal as possible.
- The "explicit out" ("if for any reason you cannot...") is critical. It lowers the social cost of a no, which makes the yes more meaningful and reduces the chance of a lukewarm letter.
- If the advisor has not been in regular contact in the months before the ask, attach a 1-page summary of what you have been doing since you last spoke. Most advisors appreciate the courtesy.
