# Ask Email — Collaborator or Project PI

**Use case:** A research collaborator on a specific project (could be the CiteCheck citation-existence verifier mini-paper, a co-authorship on an open-source contribution, or a non-thesis research project). Could be a peer who has become senior, or a PI from a research project distinct from your master's thesis.

**Send timing:** May–July 2027.

---

**To:** [Recommender's email]
**Subject:** PhD letter of recommendation — Fall 2028 cycle — for the CiteCheck (or [PROJECT]) work

---

Dear [First Name or Dr. Last Name],

I hope you are well. I am writing to ask whether you would be willing to write a letter of recommendation for my PhD CS applications in the Fall 2028 cycle. The earliest deadlines are Dec 1, 2027.

You worked with me on [SPECIFIC PROJECT — e.g., "the eyecite open-source contribution series during 2026-2027" / "the CiteCheck citation-existence verifier mini-paper that we co-shipped in Sep 2026" / "the [name of collaboration]"]. That collaboration is one of the artifacts I am pointing to in my application, and the perspective you have on it is one no other recommender on my list can give.

The PhD direction I am applying to extends that work. My research focus is **agentic retrieval-augmented generation for verifiable US case-law citations**, addressing the *Mata v. Avianca* problem where LLMs fabricate plausible-looking but non-existent legal citations. The system, called CiteCheck, combines a Bluebook-structure-aware reranker with a live-registry verification loop against the CourtListener API. The [SPECIFIC TECHNIQUE / COMPONENT from your shared work] is at the core of CiteCheck v0.1 — and the [SPECIFIC LIMITATION / EXTENSION] we discussed during the collaboration is one of the directions I want to take the work during the PhD. The full design is at https://github.com/StevenGabule/phd-computer-science.

I am asking you specifically because the [PERSONALIZE: e.g., "way we debugged the [SPECIFIC ISSUE]" / "the iterations we went through on [SPECIFIC DECISION]"] showed you a side of my research process — handling disagreement, recovering from a wrong direction, deciding when to ship vs. when to keep iterating — that committees rarely get to hear about. That is the dimension I would want a letter to focus on, more than the polished output.

I am applying to about twenty programs across US and Europe; earliest deadline Dec 1, 2027. **If you cannot write a strong letter — too little context, scheduling, or any reason — please tell me directly.** A one-line "no" is more useful than a polite yes that turns into a weak letter.

If you are willing, I have a recommender packet ready (CV, draft SOP, the specific deliverable from our collaboration, full program list with deadlines, and suggested talking points oriented around the collaboration) that I will send as soon as you confirm.

A one-line response in the next two weeks would help me plan.

Thank you.

Sincerely,
John Paul L. Gabule
johnpaullimgabule@gmail.com

## Customization checklist before sending

- [ ] Confirm address form (First Name vs. Dr. Last Name) based on existing relationship
- [ ] Replace `[SPECIFIC PROJECT]` with the actual collaboration name + dates
- [ ] Replace `[SPECIFIC TECHNIQUE / COMPONENT]` and `[SPECIFIC LIMITATION / EXTENSION]` with concrete technical references
- [ ] Replace `[PERSONALIZE: ...]` with a specific moment from the collaboration
- [ ] If the collaboration produced a public artifact (paper, GitHub repo, blog post), link to it in the email
- [ ] Verify the recipient's current affiliation
- [ ] Send Tuesday or Wednesday morning recipient-time

## Notes

- Collaborator letters are unusually strong because they speak to the unpolished side of research (iteration, recovery, decision-making) that thesis-supervisor letters and coursework letters usually do not.
- If the collaborator is junior (e.g., a recent PhD or another master's student who has since become a postdoc), the letter is sometimes weighted slightly less by committees but still credible if the technical depth is clear.
- If you have a co-authored artifact (especially something that landed publicly — a paper, an arXiv preprint, a merged PR series), reference it concretely in the email. It anchors the letter in something verifiable.
- This archetype works particularly well as your third letter, complementing a master's-thesis-advisor letter and a coursework letter. Together they cover sustained research, breadth, and process.
