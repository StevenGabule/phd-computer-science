# Ask Email — Coursework Professor

**Use case:** A professor from a key NLP / ML / IR / legal-informatics course where you did notably well and demonstrated research-style thinking. They have seen your technical depth in a specific area but not necessarily your sustained research output.

**Send timing:** May–July 2027 (earlier the better; coursework professors are easier to remind of the specific work).

---

**To:** [Recommender's institutional email]
**Subject:** PhD letter of recommendation — Fall 2028 cycle — [Course Name] alum

---

Dear Professor [Last Name],

I was a student in your [COURSE NUMBER / NAME] in [SEMESTER / YEAR], and I am writing to ask whether you would be willing to write a letter of recommendation for my PhD CS applications in the Fall 2028 cycle.

To remind you of my specific work in the course: I [PERSONALIZE: e.g., "completed the final project on [TOPIC], which I [received an A on / extended into a workshop submission / shared with you in office hours]" / "presented on [PAPER] for the [PAPER-DISCUSSION SECTION]"]. I have attached the [original deliverable / project report / paper draft] so it is easier to remember the specifics; I do not assume you would otherwise.

The PhD direction I am applying to do builds on the methodology your course exposed me to. My research focus is **agentic retrieval-augmented generation for verifiable US case-law citations** — addressing the *Mata v. Avianca* problem where LLMs fabricate plausible-looking but non-existent legal citations. The system (CiteCheck) combines a Bluebook-structure-aware reranker with a live-registry verification loop against the CourtListener API. The full design is at https://github.com/StevenGabule/phd-computer-science. The [SPECIFIC TECHNIQUE FROM YOUR COURSE — e.g., "cross-encoder reranking", "constrained decoding", "retrieval evaluation methodology"] you taught us is at the center of the method, and that is the through-line I would want a letter to be able to speak to.

I am asking about twenty programs across US and Europe; earliest deadline is Dec 1, 2027. **If you would prefer not to write a letter — too little context, scheduling, or you would not feel able to write strongly given how briefly we worked together — please tell me directly.** A one-line "no" is much more valuable to me than a vague yes.

If you are willing, I have a recommender packet ready (CV, draft SOP, the original course deliverable, the full program list with portal links, and suggested talking points) that I will send as soon as you confirm. I am happy to come to office hours / a video call to refresh on my work if that helps.

Even a one-line response in the next two weeks would help me plan my list.

Thank you for considering this.

Sincerely,
John Paul L. Gabule
johnpaullimgabule@gmail.com
[link to research blog / GitHub]

## Customization checklist before sending

- [ ] Replace `[COURSE NUMBER / NAME]` and `[SEMESTER / YEAR]` accurately
- [ ] Replace `[PERSONALIZE: ...]` with a specific memorable project / contribution
- [ ] Attach the original course deliverable (PDF) — most coursework professors will not remember the specifics without it
- [ ] Replace `[SPECIFIC TECHNIQUE FROM YOUR COURSE]` with whichever method actually appears in CiteCheck
- [ ] Verify the GitHub link is live
- [ ] Send Tuesday or Wednesday morning recipient-time

## Notes

- Coursework letters are generally weaker than research letters because they speak to a narrower window of contact. Use this archetype when you don't have enough sustained-research recommenders to fill the slots.
- Attaching the original deliverable is critical. Without it, the professor either declines or writes a generic letter; with it, they can write specifically about the work.
- If you can offer to come to office hours or a video call before they commit, do — it lets them gauge whether they remember enough to write strongly.
