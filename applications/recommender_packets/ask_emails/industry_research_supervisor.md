# Ask Email — Industry Research Supervisor

**Use case:** A research supervisor from an industry role (research engineer, research-track intern, applied scientist supervisor). They can speak to your professional research capability outside an academic context — useful to balance the recommender mix away from a purely-academic pedigree.

**Send timing:** May–July 2027.

---

**To:** [Recommender's email]
**Subject:** PhD letter of recommendation — Fall 2028 cycle — John Paul Gabule

---

Dear [First Name or Dr. Last Name — depending on relationship],

I hope you are well. I am writing to ask whether you would be willing to write a letter of recommendation for my PhD CS applications in the Fall 2028 cycle, which open in Sep 2027 with the earliest deadlines in Dec 2027.

You supervised my work at [COMPANY / TEAM] during [TIMEFRAME], when I [PERSONALIZE: e.g., "owned the [SPECIFIC PROJECT]" / "contributed to [SHIPPED ARTIFACT]" / "designed and shipped [SYSTEM]"]. That work directly motivated my decision to pursue research full-time — and the methodology I am building my PhD direction around grew out of the [PERSONALIZE: e.g., retrieval / evaluation / pipeline-design] questions we ran into during that period.

My research focus for the PhD is **agentic retrieval-augmented generation for verifiable US case-law citations** — addressing the *Mata v. Avianca* problem where LLMs fabricate citations at deployment-risk rates (Magesh et al. 2025 measured 17–33% on commercial legal AI). The system, CiteCheck, combines a Bluebook-structure-aware reranker with a live-registry verification loop against the CourtListener API. I have been building it across 2026-2027; the full design is at https://github.com/StevenGabule/phd-computer-science. The motivation is open-weight legal AI tooling for public defenders and pro-bono lawyers, who are the populations most exposed to fabricated-citation harm and least able to afford proprietary tools.

I am asking you specifically because your perspective on my research capability *in a professional setting* is one most PhD committees do not get from academic recommenders. The dimension I most need a letter to speak to is [PERSONALIZE: e.g., "shipping research code under real constraints" / "designing experiments under product timelines" / "the difference between a finished research artifact and a finished thesis chapter"]. That is what you have seen and few others have.

I am applying to about twenty programs across US and Europe; earliest deadline Dec 1, 2027. **If you would prefer not to write a letter — too little recent context, scheduling, or anything else — please tell me directly.** I would rather know now and find a different recommender than send a lukewarm letter to top-tier admissions committees.

If you are willing, I have a recommender packet ready (CV, draft SOP, accomplishments list, full program list with deadlines, suggested talking points emphasizing the professional dimension) that I will send as soon as you confirm. I can also send a 1-page summary of what I have been doing since we last worked together, if that is helpful.

A one-line response in the next two weeks would help me plan.

Thank you for considering this.

Sincerely,
John Paul L. Gabule
johnpaullimgabule@gmail.com
[link to research blog / GitHub]

## Customization checklist before sending

- [ ] Confirm whether to address as `[First Name]` (peer / close) or `Dr. [Last Name]` (formal)
- [ ] Replace `[COMPANY / TEAM]` and `[TIMEFRAME]` accurately
- [ ] Replace the three `[PERSONALIZE: ...]` blocks with specific shared experience
- [ ] If you have been out of touch for >12 months, attach a 1-paragraph "what I have been doing since" summary
- [ ] Verify the GitHub link is live
- [ ] Verify their current employer / email (people move; the address you have may be stale)

## Notes

- Industry recommenders can write surprisingly strong letters for PhD admissions if they emphasize *research* capability (not engineering capability). Coach them gently in the packet.
- The "open-weight legal AI for pro-bono lawyers" framing often resonates more with industry researchers who care about impact than the pure-methods framing does.
- If the industry recommender does not have a PhD themselves, the letter is sometimes weighted slightly less by committees. Still useful for the perspective; just be aware of the dynamic.
- If you only have one or two industry recommenders and most committees want 3 academic letters, the industry letter often goes in slot #3 to complement two academic letters.
