In May 2023, Judge P. Kevin Castel of the Southern District of New York sanctioned two attorneys for filing a brief in *Mata v. Avianca* that cited six judicial opinions that did not exist. The citations were syntactically flawless — proper Bluebook form, plausible reporters, real-looking docket numbers — but ChatGPT had hallucinated them, and neither the filing attorneys nor opposing counsel could verify the fabrication until the court did. By early 2026, Damien Charlotin's running AI Hallucinations tracker has logged roughly fifty US court orders disciplining lawyers for fabricated AI-generated authorities, and Magesh et al. (2025) — work led from the lab I am applying to — showed that even commercial legal RAG systems sold as "hallucination-free," Lexis+ AI and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research, invent or misattribute case law between 17% and 33% of the time. The legal profession is in an AI verifiability crisis with first-order consequences for sanctions, malpractice, and access to justice, and the research community has so far ceded the empirical evaluation of this problem to the same vendors it should be auditing. That is the gap I want my doctoral work to close, and I would like to do it at the lab that defined the problem.

The state of open research on legal RAG is unsatisfying in three specific ways. First, *Hallucination-Free?* (Magesh et al., 2025) was conducted on closed commercial systems with closed prompts, so no one outside the vendors can reproduce, ablate, or improve on those numbers. The audit is therefore a one-shot indictment, not a moving research benchmark. Second, the leading open legal-RAG benchmarks either evaluate retrieval quality without separately scoring citation existence and propositional support (LegalBench-RAG; Pipitone & Houir Alami, 2024), or they focus on European corpora and entity linking rather than US case-law citation verification (EL-RAG; Wankhade et al., 2026). Third, the existing evaluation framing treats a hallucinated citation as a single failure mode, when in practice it decomposes into at least three distinct errors: the cited case does not exist; it exists but does not stand for the asserted proposition; or it exists and supports the proposition but comes from a non-binding jurisdiction. Conflating these has masked which failure mode dominates in different generator families, which is exactly the question a serious audit should answer.

I propose **CiteCheck**, an open benchmark and method for verifiable US case-law citations in retrieval-augmented generation. The benchmark scores every emitted citation on three independent axes: **existence** (does the citation resolve to a real opinion in CourtListener?), **support** (does the cited passage entail the proposition the model asserts it stands for, measured by a Citation Support F1 metric that combines precision and recall against headnotes and Bluebook-anchored spans?), and **jurisdictional validity** (is the cited authority binding or merely persuasive for the forum the brief addresses?). The method is an agentic RAG pipeline that, at decode-time, calls a CitationResolver tool against a live CourtListener mirror, retracts citations that fail existence, and reranks the remainder with a faithfulness-tuned reranker that treats reporter / volume / page / court / year as a hard constraint rather than as opaque text. I want to be honest about scope: CiteCheck v1 will cover federal appellate and Supreme Court opinions, not the long tail of state trial courts where CourtListener coverage is uneven; the support axis will rely on natural-language inference over headnotes, which is a known weak proxy for the legal notion of "standing for a proposition"; and the agentic retraction step trades latency for reliability in ways some downstream uses cannot afford. These limitations are themselves research questions. Target venues for the first two papers are NLLP@EMNLP 2027 and TrustNLP@NAACL 2027, with an arXiv pre-release of the benchmark earlier to invite community contribution.

My preparation for this work is Master's-level applied AI rather than core ML theory, and I think that orientation is the right one for the problem. The skills that transfer most directly are building reproducible evaluation harnesses, working with messy legal-domain text (citation parsers, court-name normalization, jurisdictional taxonomies), fine-tuning rerankers on faithfulness signals, and the patience to do careful annotation rather than rely on synthetic labels.

Stanford RegLab is the natural and, frankly, the obvious home for this work. Professor Daniel Ho's and Professor Julian Nyarko's *A Reasoning-Focused Legal Retrieval Benchmark* (2025) is the closest existing artifact to what CiteCheck extends — it isolates retrieval quality on legal-reasoning queries in a way that LegalBench-RAG did not, and the support axis in CiteCheck (the Citation Support F1 score) is intended to be a direct, drop-in evaluation module compatible with that benchmark's query set. The companion *Hallucinating Law* line of work (Magesh et al., 2025) is the empirical baseline CiteCheck is designed to make reproducible on open systems. Beyond the citation-audit work specifically, RegLab's broader regulatory and administrative-law portfolio — work on agency rulemaking, regulatory complexity, and access-to-justice — is the right institutional context for thinking about which failure modes actually matter for public-interest legal practice, not just commercial litigation. I would hope to discuss the support-axis design with Professor Nyarko in particular, given his work on legal-language entailment in *LegalBench*, and the jurisdictional-validity axis with Professor Ho, whose administrative-law expertise is rare in CS departments. Stanford's combination of CS, Law, and HAI also gives CiteCheck access to law-school clinics and court-data partners that no independent researcher can assemble alone.

CiteCheck is the first project, not the program. The same three-axis evaluation framework generalizes to other high-stakes citation domains: medical literature in clinical decision support, statutory and regulatory citations beyond case law, and the harder problem of real-time citation to legislation that has been amended or repealed since a model's training cutoff. By year three I would expect to extend the work to multi-jurisdictional systems where the binding-versus-persuasive distinction is genuinely difficult, and by year five to the temporal-validity question in a world of continuously updated legal databases — a problem RegLab's regulatory-text work is already partly engaged with. The through-line is verifiable generation in domains where being plausibly wrong is worse than being clearly uncertain, and Stanford is where I would like to do that work.
