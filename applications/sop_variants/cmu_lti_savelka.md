A federal judge in the Southern District of New York sanctioned two attorneys in May 2023 for filing a brief in *Mata v. Avianca* citing six judicial opinions that ChatGPT had invented. The names were plausible, the Bluebook formatting was flawless, the reporter numbers looked right, and the model had even composed convincing quoted passages. What it had not done was check whether the cases existed. That is the failure mode the legal-NLP community has been studying with increasing alarm — Charlotin's running tracker logs roughly fifty US court orders involving fabricated AI authorities as of early 2026 — and Magesh et al. (2025) demonstrated that even commercial RAG-grounded legal tools, marketed as "hallucination-free," misattribute or invent case law between 17% and 33% of the time. The problem is not surface fluency. The problem is that current evaluation conflates several genuinely distinct failures into one error rate, and the legal-text infrastructure for separating them — the parsing, normalization, and annotation work — is exactly where my preparation has been heading. I want to build the open benchmark and the open method that turn this audit into a research program.

The published evaluation landscape has three gaps that hold the field back. First, *Hallucination-Free?* (Magesh et al., 2025) tested closed commercial systems with closed prompts, so its findings cannot be reproduced, ablated, or extended without vendor cooperation that no one outside the vendors has. Second, the open legal-RAG benchmarks that do exist either score retrieval quality without separately measuring citation existence and propositional support (LegalBench-RAG; Pipitone & Houir Alami, 2024), or they target European corpora and entity linking rather than US case-law verification (EL-RAG; Wankhade et al., 2026). Third — and this is the gap legal-NLP people see most clearly — a hallucinated citation is not one error type. It decomposes into at least three: the cited case does not exist; it exists but does not stand for the asserted proposition; or it exists and supports the proposition but is from a non-binding jurisdiction. Lumping these three into a single "hallucination rate" hides which failure dominates in which model family, and that masking has real consequences for both research priorities and downstream practice.

I propose **CiteCheck**, an open benchmark and method for verifiable US case-law citations in retrieval-augmented generation. The benchmark scores each emitted citation on three axes: **existence** (does the citation resolve to a real opinion in CourtListener?), **support** (does the cited passage entail the proposition the model asserts, scored against headnotes via a Citation Support F1 metric?), and **jurisdictional validity** (is the cited authority binding or persuasive for the forum the brief addresses?). The pipeline is agentic: at decode-time it calls a CitationResolver tool against a live CourtListener mirror, retracts citations failing existence, and reranks the remainder using a Bluebook-structure-aware reranker that treats reporter / volume / page / court / year as a hard parsing constraint rather than as opaque text. The eyecite citation parser from the Free Law Project does the heavy lifting on the parsing side, and one of my pre-doctoral commitments is to contribute substantively to that library so the parsing layer is reusable beyond CiteCheck. Honest scope caveats: v1 covers federal appellate and Supreme Court opinions, not the long tail of state trial courts; the support axis depends on NLI over headnotes, a known weak proxy for the legal notion of "standing for a proposition"; and the agentic retraction step trades latency for reliability. Target venues for the first two papers are NLLP@EMNLP 2027 and TrustNLP@NAACL 2027.

My preparation is Master's-level applied AI, oriented toward systems-and-evaluation work rather than core ML theory, and the orientation is the right one for the problem. See applications/artifacts_strategy.md.]`. The skills that transfer most directly are reproducible eval harnesses, legal-domain text wrangling (citation parsers, court-name normalization, jurisdictional taxonomies), reranker fine-tuning, and the patience for careful annotation over synthetic labels.

CMU LTI, and specifically the TEEL Lab, is an unusual research environment because Dr. Jaromír Šavelka holds both the CS and the JD perspectives that this problem genuinely needs. *The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Large Language Models in Zero-Shot Legal Annotation* (Šavelka, Ashley, et al., 2023) is the foundational empirical result that justifies CiteCheck's reliance on LLM-assisted annotation for the support axis — it shows that zero-shot LLM annotation on legal text is much better than the conventional wisdom suggested, which is the load-bearing assumption underneath the benchmark's data-construction strategy. Dr. Šavelka's later work, *Adding Argumentation into Evaluation of Legal Summarization* (2024), is methodologically adjacent: it argues that evaluation of legal-text generation has to be decomposed by argument structure rather than scored as a single metric, and CiteCheck's three-axis decomposition is the citation-domain analog of that same argument. Beyond the paper-level fit, the TEEL Lab is one of very few CS labs where someone can read a Bluebook citation, run an eyecite parse, and discuss the legal-evidentiary question of what it means for a case to "stand for" a proposition in the same hour. That combination — and CMU's broader strength in LLM agents through LTI and the Neubig group — is the environment CiteCheck needs.

CiteCheck is the first project, not the program. The three-axis framework — existence, support, validity — generalizes to other high-stakes citation domains: medical literature in clinical decision support, statutory and regulatory citations beyond case law, and the harder problem of citations to legislation amended or repealed since training. By year three I would extend to multi-jurisdictional systems where binding-versus-persuasive is genuinely difficult, and by year five to temporal validity in continuously updated legal databases. The through-line is verifiable generation in domains where being plausibly wrong is worse than being clearly uncertain, and the TEEL Lab's combination of legal-domain expertise and LTI's broader NLP infrastructure is where I would like to do that work.
