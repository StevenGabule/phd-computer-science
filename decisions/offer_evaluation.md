# Offer Evaluation Framework

**Purpose:** When admission offers arrive in March-April 2028, evaluate them with structured criteria rather than vibes. This is likely the biggest decision in the 18-month plan and the next 5 years.

**Use timing:** January-April 2028 as offers come in. Re-read this file every time you receive an offer.

---

## The decision shape

You will have, in approximate order:
1. ~5-10 rejections (early to mid-cycle)
2. ~2-5 interview invitations (Jan-Feb 2028)
3. ~1-4 acceptance offers (Feb-Apr 2028)
4. ~0-2 waitlist offers
5. One offer you accept (April 2028)

The vast majority of applicants land at a program that wasn't their top choice on day 1. That's normal. The goal of this framework is to make sure the choice you make is good for *you*, not for an imagined ranking.

---

## Weighted criteria (the framework)

For each offer, score 1-10 on each criterion. Multiply by weight. Sum.

| Criterion | Weight | What to evaluate |
|---|---|---|
| Advisor fit | 40% | Research direction overlap; advising style; reachability; lab culture |
| Funding stability | 20% | Multi-year guarantee; summer funding; conference travel budget |
| Cohort and lab culture | 15% | Current students' satisfaction; collaborative vs. competitive |
| Program structure | 10% | Qualifier exam; time-to-degree; breadth requirements; teaching load |
| Geographic / personal fit | 10% | Cost of living; partner/family; weather; community |
| Brand / network | 5% | Program reputation in your subfield; alumni network |

**Why these weights:**
- Advisor dominates because you'll spend more time with them than anyone over the next 4-6 years
- Funding stability matters because financial stress destroys research productivity
- Cohort matters because peers are who you actually do research with day-to-day
- Program structure matters less than people think — most US PhD programs have similar shapes
- Geographic fit matters more than people admit in pre-application but more than they realize during the PhD
- Brand matters but is the single most overweighted criterion in initial impressions; the actual signal is what your lab outputs, not what's on your degree

---

## Criterion 1: Advisor fit (40%)

The single most important factor. A weak fit with a famous advisor is worse than a strong fit with an unknown one.

### Questions to ask:
1. **Research direction:** does the advisor's recent work (last 2 years) overlap with what you want to do?
2. **Advising style:** hands-on (weekly meetings, close supervision) vs. hands-off (monthly, you set the pace)? Match your needs.
3. **Reachability:** how often does the advisor respond to email? to Slack? to door-knocks?
4. **Lab culture:** social / collaborative or solo / competitive?
5. **Funding source:** RA-funded by advisor (more secure) or TA-funded by department (more variable)?
6. **Track record:** how many of their previous PhDs have graduated? What did they go on to do?
7. **Reputation as a mentor:** what do current and former students say?

### Red flags:
- Advisor says "I'm not sure if I'll take a student this year"
- Multiple recent students transferred out or took longer than expected to graduate
- Advisor's most recent published work is 3+ years old
- Conflict between what the advisor describes the lab as and what current students describe
- "I'll take you but only if you work on X" (X is something you don't want to work on)

### Green flags:
- Multiple recent students with publications
- Lab has both senior (year 4-5) and junior (year 1-2) students — suggests consistent intake
- Recent paper from the lab is in your area of interest
- Advisor's framing of your direction matches yours (or improves it)

### Scoring guide:
- 10: dream advisor, exact research overlap, students rave about them, clear funding
- 7-8: strong fit, good track record, minor question marks
- 5: acceptable, you can imagine 5 years there
- 3: poor fit, you'd be paddling against the current
- 0-2: probably wrong; consider declining

---

## Criterion 2: Funding stability (20%)

PhD financial precarity destroys research productivity. Look beyond the year-1 stipend.

### Questions to evaluate:
1. **Multi-year guarantee:** is funding guaranteed for the full program (5-6 years) or just year 1?
2. **Stipend amount:** see `financial/stipend_planning.md` for benchmarks
3. **Stipend duration:** 9-month (TA-dependent for summer) vs. 12-month
4. **Summer funding:** guaranteed or competitive?
5. **Tuition coverage:** typically 100% for US PhDs; verify
6. **Health insurance:** included? what tier?
7. **Conference travel:** typical budget per year ($500-3,000)
8. **Computing resources:** shared cluster access? personal allocation? Colab-only?
9. **Top-up potential:** does the program allow external fellowships (NSF GRFP, etc.) to top up?

### Red flags:
- "Funding contingent on satisfactory progress" without clear criteria
- Year 1 only guaranteed; later years subject to RA availability
- No summer funding; must apply year-by-year
- "TA load" of 20+ hours/week during research-active years

### Green flags:
- Multi-year guaranteed funding (5-year RA + tuition covered)
- Stipend at or above local cost-of-living benchmarks
- Health insurance fully covered
- Conference travel budget that allows 2+ trips per year

### Scoring guide:
- 10: 5-6 year guarantee, 12-month stipend at or above COL benchmark, full benefits
- 7-8: multi-year guarantee with minor caveats
- 5: year-1 secure, later years probable but not guaranteed
- 3: precarious funding, year-by-year applications
- 0-2: red flags; decline unless this is your only offer

---

## Criterion 3: Cohort and lab culture (15%)

You'll do research with your peers more than with your advisor.

### Questions to ask:
1. **Lab size:** what's the active student count? (4-8 is typical; 15+ may dilute attention)
2. **Recent grads:** what did they go on to do? Industry research, faculty, other?
3. **Lab social:** do they get coffee together? lunch? weekly meetings?
4. **Collaborative culture:** do students co-author? help each other on bugs?
5. **Inclusive culture:** how diverse is the lab (gender, nationality, background)?
6. **Conflict patterns:** are there obvious cliques? Has there been recent attrition?
7. **External engagement:** does the lab co-author with other groups? attend external workshops together?

### How to assess (during visit days or interview weekends):
- Ask 3-5 current students the same question: "what's the hardest unexpected thing about being a PhD student here?"
- Attend a lab meeting (most programs allow this)
- Ask about the advisor in casual settings (when the advisor isn't present)
- Look at recent papers' co-author lists for collaboration patterns

### Red flags:
- Students seem isolated or wary
- High recent attrition (multiple students transferred out)
- Lab meetings feel performative rather than collaborative
- Senior students don't help junior students

### Green flags:
- Students openly discuss their work with each other
- Lab attends conferences together
- Cross-lab collaborations are common
- Students from different cohorts maintain relationships

### Scoring guide:
- 10: vibrant collaborative lab, you can imagine years there
- 7-8: solid culture, no major concerns
- 5: acceptable but not energizing
- 3: noticeable concerns; the day-to-day might be lonely
- 0-2: actively bad culture; will damage you

---

## Criterion 4: Program structure (10%)

US PhD programs are more similar than different in structure. The differences that matter:

### Questions to ask:
1. **Qualifier exam:** when (year 1-2)? written + oral or just written? pass rate?
2. **Time to degree:** median for the program? for your advisor's students?
3. **Breadth requirements:** how many courses outside your area required?
4. **Teaching load:** how many semesters of TA required?
5. **Thesis structure:** monograph or stapled papers (most CS PhDs)?
6. **Funding model:** RA-only (advisor funds via grants) or RA + TA mix?

### What varies meaningfully:
- **Coursework load:** Stanford / CMU have higher first-year coursework; some programs let you waive courses with prior experience.
- **TA requirement:** ranges from 0 (rare) to 4 semesters (more common). Each TA semester costs ~2 months of research time.
- **Qualifier:** some programs have a survey-paper format (less stressful, more learning); others have a high-stakes written exam (more time-consuming, more pressure).
- **Internships:** some programs encourage 1-2 summer internships at industry labs; others see them as a distraction.

### Scoring guide:
- 10: program structure aligns with how you want to work
- 7-8: minor inconveniences (more coursework than ideal, e.g.)
- 5: program will work but the structure adds friction
- 3: significant structural barriers to your preferred working style
- 0-2: program will fight you the whole way

---

## Criterion 5: Geographic / personal fit (10%)

The next 5-6 years of your life.

### Questions to evaluate:
1. **Cost of living:** see `financial/stipend_planning.md`
2. **Partner / family considerations:** can your partner work / study there? Are children supported?
3. **Weather:** seasonal-affective patterns matter for some people; sunlight matters
4. **Community:** is there a cultural / religious / hobby community you need?
5. **Distance from home:** flight cost + time for visits
6. **Quality of life:** good food, walkability, public transit, outdoors access

### Things you'll underweight in spring 2028 but overweight by year 2:
- Weather and seasonal depression
- Distance from family
- Whether your partner is happy in the city

### Things you'll overweight in spring 2028 but underweight by year 2:
- Trendiness of the city
- Proximity to "the action" in tech
- Whether your friends are there

### Scoring guide:
- 10: you'd want to live here even without the PhD
- 7-8: comfortable, no major concerns
- 5: workable, some adjustments needed
- 3: significant friction (long-distance from partner, climate mismatch, etc.)
- 0-2: actively dread the move

---

## Criterion 6: Brand / network (5%)

The criterion that gets overweighted in initial reactions.

### What "brand" actually buys you:
- Pre-screening reputation when you apply for postdocs / faculty jobs (real but smaller than people think)
- Easier access to industry research roles (real for top-5 programs; modest for top-30)
- Stronger alumni network in specific cities (e.g., MIT in Cambridge, Stanford in Bay Area, UMich in Detroit)

### What "brand" does NOT buy you:
- A better day-to-day PhD experience (more about advisor and cohort)
- Faster time to degree (more about advisor)
- Stronger publications (more about your work and advisor connections)
- Job placement if your work isn't strong (brand doesn't compensate for weak research)

### Scoring guide:
- 10: top-5 program in your subfield, in a city with strong alumni network
- 7-8: top-15 program with reasonable network
- 5: top-30 program
- 3: regional program with weak external recognition
- 0-2: program with active reputation problems

## The composite score

Multiply scores by weights; sum. Compare across offers.

| Program | Advisor (40%) | Funding (20%) | Cohort (15%) | Structure (10%) | Geographic (10%) | Brand (5%) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stanford | 9 × 0.40 = 3.60 | 9 × 0.20 = 1.80 | 8 × 0.15 = 1.20 | 7 × 0.10 = 0.70 | 6 × 0.10 = 0.60 | 10 × 0.05 = 0.50 | **8.40** |
| CMU | 9 × 0.40 = 3.60 | 9 × 0.20 = 1.80 | 9 × 0.15 = 1.35 | 8 × 0.10 = 0.80 | 8 × 0.10 = 0.80 | 9 × 0.05 = 0.45 | **8.80** |
| (etc.) | | | | | | | |

**The numerical score is a tiebreaker, not a decision-maker.** Use the framework to surface considerations; use your gut for the final call.

## Anti-patterns

### "I should pick the highest-ranked"
Rankings are aggregate; your experience is individual. Use rankings as a coarse filter (top-30 vs. top-100), not as a final tie-breaker.

### "I should pick where I have the most prestigious offer"
Prestige is a 5% factor in this framework. If a 5% factor is your deciding criterion, you don't have enough information about the other 95%.

### "I should pick what looks best on a CV"
Five years from now, your CV will show one PhD program. The 5 years of life *inside* the PhD will be very different across programs.

### "I should pick where my partner's job opportunities are best"
Defensible IF both of you have explicitly discussed and agreed. Dangerous IF one of you is silently sacrificing.

### "I should defer the decision; I'll regret it less if I sleep on it"
You should sleep on it. But "deferring" past April 15 risks losing the offer to the waitlist. Don't fall into infinite deliberation.

## Visit days and interviews

Most US PhD programs hold "visit days" or "open houses" in March 2028 for admitted students. Attend if possible (programs usually cover travel cost).

**What to do at visit days:**
- Meet your prospective advisor at least once 1-on-1
- Attend a lab meeting if invited
- Ask current students the same 3 questions (compare answers across programs)
- Walk around the city; can you see yourself living here?
- Notice your gut reaction. The body knows things the spreadsheet doesn't.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't be performative ("trying to impress" — they've already admitted you)
- Don't reveal which other programs you're considering (unless asked directly)
- Don't make a decision on the visit day (sleep on it for at least a week)

## The waitlist scenario

If you're on a waitlist at a program you'd accept over your current best offer:

1. Tell the waitlist program clearly: "I'd accept if admitted; please advise on the timeline."
2. Keep your current best offer alive until April 15.
3. Most waitlist movement happens between April 1-15.
4. If you accept your current best offer and then the waitlist program offers, you can withdraw your acceptance (uncommon but ethical). Notify the first program immediately.

---

## The rejection scenario (all programs)

This is rare but possible. See `decisions/rejection_protocol.md` for the full plan. Short version:

1. Don't panic. PhD admissions has high variance year-to-year.
2. Don't take rejection personally. Many strong applicants don't get in on the first cycle.
3. Reflect on what was missing (sometimes it's the artifacts; sometimes it's the fit; sometimes it's just numbers).
4. Plan for the next cycle: industry research role for a year, or a more focused master's thesis, or...
5. The 18-month plan's artifacts (paper, benchmark, code) persist. They strengthen the next cycle's application.

---

## The "I accepted but I'm not sure" scenario

After accepting, doubt is normal. Some checks:

- The doubt is about the program, not the PhD path itself? → normal; revisit in 6 months.
- The doubt is about the PhD path itself? → talk to a therapist and former PhDs; this is bigger than one decision.
- The doubt is specifically about the advisor? → in many programs you can switch advisors in year 1-2; verify the policy at your program.

You don't have to commit to a 6-year identity on April 15. You're committing to start; the program will reveal itself.

---

## Final decision protocol

1. **April 1:** start ranking offers. Use the framework above.
2. **April 5:** finalize ranking. Discuss with partner / family / trusted advisor.
3. **April 10:** make the call. Notify the chosen program.
4. **April 12:** notify rejected programs (politely; many programs share notes on candidates).
5. **April 15:** US PhD common-reply date. Final.

After accepting:
- Email your future advisor with a brief note saying you've accepted and you're excited.
- Plan the move (housing, moving logistics).
- Take a real break in May-July before the program starts.
- Re-read this file before you start year 1 — the criteria you used will inform how you evaluate your experience.

---

## A closing thought

The choice of PhD program is reversible (transfers happen, sometimes), but expensive to reverse. Choose like you mean it; commit like the choice was right; revisit only with substantial new information.

Most people end up happy with their choice once they're 6 months in. The differences between top programs are real but smaller than initial impressions suggest. The advisor, cohort, and your own work will dominate the experience — not the institution name.

Use the framework; trust your gut for the final call; move forward without regret.
